What is the challenge?

Architectural style classification is challenging for a few reasons. Style boundaries are blurry — styles evolved gradually and borrowed elements from each other. Some styles have high intra-class variation: from small living houses to large government buildings. Street photos add noise, shadows, and occlusions.

Dataset & Method

Architectural Styles Dataset — 9,761 images across 24 classes. Four CNN architectures were trained using transfer learning on ImageNet weights: ResNet18, ResNet50, EfficientNet-B0, and ConvNeXt-Tiny. Training included data augmentation, L2 regularization, and early stopping. Each model was run three times — results are averaged for stability.

What are the insights?

Best performing

Styles with distinctive visual features — Achaemenid, Ancient Egyptian, Gothic — achieved F1 above 0.86. Minimal overlap with other styles makes them easier to classify.

Hardest to classify

Edwardian, Bauhaus, and Beaux-Arts showed the lowest F1. High intra-class variability and shared visual elements with neighboring styles make them objectively difficult.

Confusion patterns

Biggest confusion between International and Bauhaus (19 errors) and Palladian and Georgian (15 errors) — historically related styles with similar visual language.

Limitations & Future Work

Limitations

Class imbalance — Queen Anne has 503 training samples, Chicago School only 194. High intra-class variability within some styles. Dataset bias toward certain regions and eras.

Future Work

Separate hyperparameter tuning per architecture. Larger and more balanced dataset. Exploring transformer-based models such as Vision Transformer for comparison.
